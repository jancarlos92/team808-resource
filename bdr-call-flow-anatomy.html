Anatomy of a Cold Call

Every call follows the same skeleton: earn the right to ask a question, pick a discovery path, convert pain into urgency, and close on the calendar. Master the structure first — the words come later.

1
First 10 seconds

Introduction

Goal: Earn 30 more seconds

Open with a temperament check — a simple "how are you?" tells you in two seconds what kind of call this is going to be. Rushed, friendly, guarded, distracted — their tone sets your pace before you've said anything about why you called.

"Hey [Name], this is [You] over at Sage Intacct — how are you doing today?"

If they ask "how are you?" back, that's your in. Answer like a real person — one quick, human beat about your day. This is a pattern interrupt: nobody cold calling ever actually answers that question, so being briefly human breaks the telemarketer script in their head and makes you a person instead of a pitch.

"Honestly? Doing great — just got my second coffee in, so you caught me at peak performance. I appreciate you asking."

Then transition into a permission-based opener — honest about the cold call, asking for a small, specific slice of time. Permission earns you a listener instead of a hostage.

"Look, I'll be upfront with you — this is a cold call. Can I take 30 seconds to tell you why I'm reaching out, and then you can decide if it's worth continuing?"
2
The bridge to discovery

Pain Prop or Value Prop

Goal: Give them a reason the question matters

Before you can ask a discovery question, you have to justify it. Lead with a pain prop (a specific problem companies like theirs face) or a value prop (a specific outcome we deliver). This is one or two sentences — not a pitch. It exists only to make your discovery question feel relevant instead of random.

"The reason I'm calling — we work with a lot of [industry] finance teams whose close drags past day ten because everything lives in spreadsheets outside the GL..."

Notice the structure: the pain prop leads directly into the discovery question. It's a ramp, not a speech.

3
The fork in the road

Discovery Question — Pick Your Path

Your discovery question should be topical but able to whittle down to a direction. Start broad enough that they can answer easily, but pointed enough that any answer gives you somewhere to go. There are three paths — every one of them should point back to something Sage Intacct specifically solves.

Path 1

The Software Path

Ask about their tech stack to hunt for pain inside it. What GL are they on? What's bolted around it? Where do spreadsheets fill the gaps?

Aging systems, heavy Excel workarounds, and disconnected tools are all pain you can name.

"What are you running for your accounting system today — and what's living outside of it in spreadsheets?"
Path 2

The Accounting Process Path

Ask about a general finance process every team runs: month-end close, reconciliations, or intercompany transactions / multi-entity consolidations.

Slow closes and manual eliminations are universal pains we automate.

"How long does your close typically take — and how much of the consolidation is still manual?"
Strongest Path 3

The Industry / Operations Path

Ask about something unique to how their business runs. This proves you understand their world — and it's the hardest for them to brush off.

Example: hotels have to break down how rooms are booked through OTAs (online travel agencies) — each channel with its own fees, remittance timing, and reconciliation headache.

"How is your team handling revenue breakdown across your OTA channels versus direct bookings?"

Whichever path you take, the destination is the same: surface a pain that maps to what we do — multi-entity consolidation, automation of manual processes, real-time dimensional reporting, and connected finance operations.

4
Pain → business impact

The Urgency Bridge

Goal: Make the pain expensive to ignore

A pain point alone doesn't book meetings — the cost of the pain does. The urgency bridge connects what they told you to its business impact, then to how solving it helps. Three moves:

MoveWhat you're doingSounds like
1. Reflect Repeat the pain in their words so they hear themselves say it. "So your team is closing the books in fifteen days and half of that is chasing spreadsheets..."
2. Amplify Attach the business impact — time, headcount, decisions delayed, growth blocked, audit risk. "...which means leadership is making decisions on numbers that are two weeks stale, every month."
3. Resolve State plainly how this changes with us — outcome first, not features. "Teams like yours are cutting close time in half and getting real-time visibility by entity, location, and channel. That's exactly what this conversation would cover."
5
Calendar or nothing

The Close

Goal: A confirmed time + a backup

Don't ask if they want to meet — direct them to their calendar and ask when. Offer at least two days and a morning or afternoon choice so they're picking between options, not deciding yes/no.

"Do me a favor and pull up your calendar — how does Tuesday or Thursday look? Better morning or afternoon?"

Always lock a backup time. Meetings slip. A backup keeps the deal alive without a new cold call.

"Perfect, Thursday at 10. And just in case something comes up on your end — would the same time Friday work as a backup?"
Hard rule Never say the meeting is with a "product specialist." It is always with a colleague. "I'd love to set up time with a colleague of mine who works with [industry] finance teams every day." Specialist language signals a sales demo; colleague language signals a conversation.
Pre-dial mental checklist
  • Intro: Temperament check ("how are you?") → human beat if they ask back → permission-based opener for 30 seconds.
  • Prop: One pain or value statement that ramps into my question.
  • Path: I've picked my discovery path (software / process / industry) before I dial — industry first when I know their business.
  • Bridge: Reflect the pain → amplify the business impact → resolve with the outcome.
  • Close: Two days, morning or afternoon, backup time locked, meeting is with a colleague.
